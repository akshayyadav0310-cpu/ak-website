Master Thesis Research at TUM

November 2024 - July 2025 | TUM Chair of Power Transmission and Distribution

My master's thesis investigates the critical impact of increasing inverter-based resources on short-circuit behavior in low-voltage isolated grids. This research addresses one of the most pressing challenges in modern power systems as we transition from traditional synchronous generators to inverter-dominated grids.

Research Focus

The study focuses on "Dynamic Modeling and Comparative Analysis of Short-Circuit Responses in Low-Voltage Isolated Grids: From Synchronous Generators to Inverter Dominance." Using DIgSILENT PowerFactory for dynamic modeling and simulation, I analyze and compare the performance of various technologies including synchronous generators, grid-following and grid-forming inverters, and synchronous condensers.

Key Objectives

The research aims to enhance short-circuit capability by defining microgrid scenarios, extending dynamic models to represent their short-circuit characteristics, and analyzing critical factors such as fault behavior, generator contributions, and optimal sizing of battery inverters or synchronous condensers.

Expected Outcomes

This research will contribute valuable insights into optimizing short-circuit contributions in isolated grids, which is essential for the reliable operation of future power systems with high renewable energy penetration.

The Future of Grid-Forming Inverters

Published on December 15, 2024 by Akshay Yadav

Grid-forming inverters represent a paradigm shift in how we approach power system stability and control. Unlike traditional grid-following inverters that rely on a strong grid reference, grid-forming inverters can establish and maintain their own voltage and frequency references, making them ideal for islanded operation and weak grid conditions.

Understanding Grid-Forming Technology

The fundamental difference between grid-following and grid-forming inverters lies in their control philosophy. Grid-following inverters act as current sources, injecting power into an established grid, while grid-forming inverters act as voltage sources, capable of creating and maintaining the grid itself.

Key Advantages

Grid-forming inverters offer several advantages including improved system stability, black-start capability, and enhanced fault ride-through performance. These characteristics make them particularly valuable in microgrids and systems with high renewable penetration.

Challenges and Solutions

Despite their advantages, grid-forming inverters face challenges including increased complexity, higher costs, and the need for sophisticated control algorithms. Research is ongoing to address these challenges and make the technology more accessible.

As we move towards a more distributed and renewable energy future, grid-forming inverters will play a crucial role in maintaining system stability and reliability.

Short-Circuit Analysis in Microgrids

Published on November 28, 2024 by Akshay Yadav

The increasing adoption of microgrids presents unique challenges for short-circuit analysis and protection. Traditional protection schemes designed for utility-scale systems may not be adequate for the dynamic and diverse nature of microgrids.

Unique Characteristics of Microgrid Faults

Microgrid short-circuit behavior differs significantly from traditional power systems due to the presence of inverter-based resources, which have current-limiting capabilities that affect fault current magnitude and duration.

Protection Challenges

The bi-directional power flow, varying short-circuit levels, and operational mode changes (grid-connected vs. islanded) create complex protection requirements that traditional schemes struggle to address.

Modern Solutions

Adaptive protection schemes, communication-based protection, and advanced relay settings are being developed to address these challenges. My research focuses on optimizing these solutions for real-world applications.

DIgSILENT PowerFactory: Best Practices

Published on November 10, 2024 by Akshay Yadav

DIgSILENT PowerFactory is a powerful tool for power system analysis, but maximizing its potential requires understanding best practices and avoiding common pitfalls. Here are insights from my extensive experience with the software.

Model Building Best Practices

Start with a clear system understanding and build models incrementally. Use consistent naming conventions and organize your network hierarchically for better maintainability.

Simulation Setup

Proper initialization is crucial for convergence. Pay attention to load flow settings, initialization methods, and time step selection for dynamic simulations.

Advanced Features

Leverage PowerFactory's scripting capabilities for repetitive tasks and automated studies. Use DPL (DIgSILENT Programming Language) for custom models and complex analysis workflows.

Performance Optimization

For large networks, consider using equivalent models for distant areas and optimize solver settings based on your specific analysis requirements.

These practices have significantly improved my simulation efficiency and result accuracy throughout my research and professional work.
